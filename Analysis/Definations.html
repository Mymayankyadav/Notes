Limits of functions

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be metric spaces; suppose \(E \subset X \), \(f\) maps \(E\) into \(Y\), and \(p\) is a limit point of \(E\). We write \(f \to q \) as \(x \to p\), or $$\lim_{x \to p}f(x)=q$$ if there is a point \(q \in Y\) with the following property: For every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that $$d_Y(f(x),q) < \varepsilon $$ for all points \(x \in E\) for which $$0 < d_X(x,p) < \delta$$

Continuous functions

Function is said to continuous iff $$\lim_{x \to p}f(x)=f(p)$$

Uniformly continuous

let \(f\) be a mapping of a metric space \(X\) into metric space \(Y\). We say that \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(X\) if for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists \(\delta > 0 \) such that $$d_Y(f(p),f(q)) < \varepsilon$$ for all \(p\) and \(q\) in \(X\) for which \(d_X(p,q) < \delta \)
